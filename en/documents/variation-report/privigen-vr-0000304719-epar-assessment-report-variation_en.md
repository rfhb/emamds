## 2. Scientific discussion

## 2.1. Introduction

## 2.1.1. Problem statement

## Disease or condition

Measles is a preventable, highly contagious, acute febrile viral illness. Following exposure, approximately 90% of susceptible individuals will develop measles. The period of contagiousness is estimated to be from 5 days before the appearance of the rash to 4 days afterward. The illness is airborne and is readily transmitted in public spaces, even in the absence of physical person-toperson contact. One or more complications occur in approximately 30% of measles cases (Gastanaduy et al, 2021). Complications of measles can be debilitating and have serious consequences such as secondary infections of diarrhoea and pneumonia, measles encephalitis, and other neurological syndromes such as acute disseminated encephalomyelitis or subacute sclerosing panencephalitis, including death. The risk of complications is increased in resource-limited settings, where the case fatality rate is 4% to 10%. It remains an important cause of global mortality and morbidity and accounts for about 100,000 deaths annually despite the availability of an effective vaccine (Kondamudi and Waymack, 2023).

## State the claimed therapeutic indication

The proposed indication was:

Measles pre-/post exposure prophylaxis for susceptible adults, children and adolescents (018 years) in whom active immunisation is contraindicated or not advised.

Consideration should also be given to official recommendations on intravenous human immunoglobulin use in measles of pre-/post-exposure prophylaxis and active immunisation.

## Epidemiology and risk factors

The epidemiology of measles is variable across the globe and is related to immunisation levels achieved in a particular region. Before implementing widespread vaccination programmes, measles accounted for an estimated 2.6 million deaths. Outbreaks of measles have occurred in resourcerich settings where vaccination uptake has declined, allowing for transmission of imported measles virus from unvaccinated and infected travellers (Paules, 2019; Mahase, 2023; Minta, 2023) and the World Health Organization (WHO) reported that approximately 134,200 deaths (15 deaths/hour) occurred in 2015 due to measles. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), there were 372 cases in 2018 and 764 cases through May of 2019 in the USA.

In 2010, all 53 countries in the WHO European Region (EUR) confirmed their commitment to eliminate measles as a priority in the European Vaccine Action Plan 2015- 2020 (Zimmerman, 2019). Between 2009 and 2017, efforts to improve vaccination coverage were successful, with 90% of individuals in the region receiving a second vaccine dose. As a result, by the end of 2017, 70% of EUR countries were verified to have eliminated endemic measles. However, in 2018, there was a subsequent increase in measles (89.5 cases per million population) due to large outbreaks in several European countries including some countries that had achieved elimination (Zimmerman, 2019). In 2018, more than 82,000 cases were reported in Europe. Eleven countries (Albania, France, Georgia, Greece, Israel, Italy, Kyrgyzstan, Romania, Russia, Serbia, and Ukraine) reported

## Management

While vaccination is the most effective means to prevent the spread of measles in the vulnerable populations identified, there is a therapeutic need for alternative therapies that can protect these patients upon measles exposure especially for those whom vaccinations are not advised or ineffective. As these patients may not respond adequately to vaccinations due to developing or weakened immune systems, making intravenous immunoglobulin G (IVIG) an important option for prophylaxis and treatment. Specifically, IVIG, with a minimum neutralizing antibody titer for measles virus, is a safe and effective alternative to address this unmet medical need.

The practice of passive immunisation against measles has been used since 1920s (Haas, 1926). Polyclonal immunoglobulins are administered parenterally to susceptible individuals, who have been in contact with an infectious case of measles, to prevent the onset of disease (Keller, 2000; Young, 2014). Both intramuscular (IM) and intravenous products are in use. Immunoglobulin brands used for measles prevention in susceptible individuals vary from country to country, and present with variable level of measle-specific antibodies reflecting circulating antibody levels in the donating population. In some countries, minimum neutralizing antibody concentrations are regulated (Guideline on clinical evaluation of vaccines, EMEA/CHMP/VWP/164653/05 Rev. 1). The effectiveness of post-exposure prophylaxis against measles with immunoglobulins is generally accepted (Montroy, 2025; Young, 2014).

## 2.2. Quality aspects

## 2.1. Description of the Change

## Background regarding Measles pre-/post exposure prophylaxis

In December 2021, EMA released a revised Guideline on the core summary of product characteristics for IVIGs. The revised Guideline includes the indication 'Measles pre-/post exposure prophylaxis for susceptible adults, children and adolescents (0-18 years) in whom active immunization is contraindicated or not advised.' The additional indication is a class indication attainable via the core SmPC guideline for IVIGs (Real World Evidence) without further clinical programs. Dosage and further measles information is retrievable from the mentioned guideline.

## Anti-Measles antibody by neutralization test (NT) method

The anti-measles antibody content in the IgPro10 final product is measured with the neutralization test (NT). Infection with measles virus leads to microscopically detectable changes in susceptible Vero cell cultures (cytopathic effect (CPE)). Anti-measles specific antibodies can neutralize the virus and thereby inhibit CPE. In the measles NT, serial dilutions of antibodies are made and incubated with a defined quantity of measles virus. Following the incubation, CPE is determined in the light microscope. The 50% neutralization dose (ND50) is calculated. It is defined as the sample dilution, at which the CPE is inhibited in 50% of investigated test units.

As standard, an in-house immunoglobulin solution is used that has been calibrated against the US/CBER reference.

## Results

Following review of the search results, 9 important and relevant publications were identified; confirmed cases of measles were used as an effectiveness measure. These publications reported on the effectiveness of immunoglobulin G (IG) when administered as post-exposure prophylaxis for measles. In publications where an effective dose was evaluated, the general recommendation for measles post-exposure prophylaxis was 400 mg/kg IV administered as a single dose. Most publications did not include reports of adverse events following IG administration. The results from the above-mentioned published studies are summarized below.

Caymaz and colleagues evaluated the effectiveness of IVIG administered to infants following exposure to measles. IVIG (0.4 g/kg) was administered to 187 infants &lt; 6 months of age within the first 6 to 10 days following exposure to measles. The infants were monitored for rash and fever for up to 28 days after dosing. The infants were divided into 2 groups: those who received IVIG at 6 days and those who received it later. These groups were compared according to the development of measles. Only 2 of 187 infants developed measles following IVIG prophylaxis (Caymaz, 2025).

A second study in infants assessed the effectiveness of 400 mg/kg IVIG as post-exposure prophylaxis (Kohlmaier, 2021). 63 of 65 (96.9%) infants had IVIG administered as post-exposure prophylaxis. None of the infants with IVIG post-exposure prophylaxis contracted measles or symptoms suggestive of measles, but both infants who did not receive postexposure prophylaxis became infected.

In a large study, data were obtained on all cases of measles notified in New South Wales, Australia over approximately a 3-month period. 57 cases of measles were reported and 1760 measles contacts were identified, of which 553 were classified as susceptible. Contacts identified as susceptible were offered post-exposure prophylactic immunization with either measles, mumps, rubella (MMR) vaccine within 3 days of exposure, or IG within 7 days of exposure. The effectiveness of receiving either MMR or IG as prophylaxis compared to no prophylaxis in susceptible contacts was 83.3% (95% CI: 27-96). The effectiveness of prophylaxis with IG alone was 75.8% (CI: 0-94) and for MMR was 100% (Sheppeard, 2009).

## 3. Benefit-Risk Balance

## 3.1. Therapeutic Context

## 3.1.1. Disease or condition

Measles is a preventable, highly contagious, acute febrile viral illness. Following exposure, approximately 90% of susceptible individuals will develop measles. The period of contagiousness is estimated to be from 5 days before the appearance of the rash to 4 days afterward. The illness is airborne and is readily transmitted in public spaces, even in the absence of physical person-toperson contact.

## 3.1.2. Available therapies and unmet medical need

While vaccination is the most effective means to prevent the spread of measles in the vulnerable populations identified, there is a therapeutic need for alternative therapies that can protect these patients upon measles exposure especially for those whom vaccinations are not advised or contraindicated.

## 3.2. Benefit-risk assessment and discussion

The applied indication for Privigen was ' measles pre-/post-exposure prophylaxis for susceptible adults, children and adolescents (0 to 18 years) in whom active immunisation is contraindicated or not advised.
